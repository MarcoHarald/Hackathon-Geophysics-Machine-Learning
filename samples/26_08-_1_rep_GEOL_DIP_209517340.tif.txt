Interval 6690 - 10032 ft. |

SUMMARY

Dipmeter processing for structural interpretation was performed over the interval 6690 -
10032 ft. on Well 26/8-1, U.K.C.S. One main logging pass and a short repeat interval
were recorded with a Schlumberger SHDT dipmeter tool. The correlation parameters used
for final processing were 4.0 ft. x 2.0 ft. x 60° oriented with respect to the borehole.
These parameters appear to give excellent results for structural interpretation as well as
an indication of many stratigraphic features.

High quality results were obtained throughout the logged interval, revealing a general
structural dip of roughly 7° to the southeast. Several intervals exhibit slight variations in
dip azimuth and/or magnitude; the structural dip of each of these individual intervals has
been evaluated.

The high quality of the data and the appearance of numerous’ sedimentary dip patterns
within the individual sands suggest channel orientation and sediment transport direction
could probably be derived from a finer-resolution stratigraphic processing and structural dip
removal. Also, borehole elipticity is noted in several of the lower sands which could be
borehole breakout resulting from differential horizontal stresses. If so, a breakout-
orientation plot could provide useful data on the orientation of tectonic stresses.

DIPMETER DATA BASE ~

The digital data used for this project consisted of a Schlumberger CSU field tape of SHDT
data; no other data or information was made available. The main logging pass covered the
interval 6676 - 10032 ft. with a repeat section. Only data from the main logging run was
used in this study.

DATA QUALITY AND CORRECTIONS

The studied section of the 26/8-1 well was drilled with a conductive seawater-polymer
mud and an 8.5" bit. The wellbore is nearly vertical, being deviated ta the northwest at
2 degrees at the top of the logged interval, building to nearly 6 degrees at 9700 ft.

Above 8080 ft. the borehole is in-gauge in the sand intervals and shows some minor
washout in the shales. The dipmeter calipers show severe borehole enlargement and

_elipticity between 8080 and 9130 ft. The borehole improves below 9130 ft. but still

exhibits a number of thin wash-outs. There is also some evidence of tectonically-oriented
borehole breakout in the sands below 9485 ft. Despite the intervals of borehole
eniargement and rugosity, the dipmeter pads appeared to maintain goad electrical contact
with the formation, resulting in good-quality data even in the intervals of enlarged borehole.

Cable tension was fairly consistent and there is no evidence of significant tool sticking.

.Accelerometer corrections were applied to the data prior to dip correlation. A magnetic

declination of 5.867° W was applied during ResTech processing. The dipmeter sonde did
not rotate during the logging run. ‘

DIP COMPUTATIONS

Dipmeter correlations are accomplished by digitally correlating a specified depth interval,
(“correlation interval") of resistivity data around the well bore between each of the
orientated electrode measurements. A plane is fixed by these correlations and reoriented
in space by subtracting the tool deviation and direction vectors.

During the correlation process, the distance up and down the borehole that is searched for
correlations at each depth interval is controlled by a specified "search angle". After
correlations are completed at one depth level, the data is indexed or stepped a certain
depth increment ("step distance”), and another round of correlations is then made.

Mathematically, a minimum of three displacement correlations is sufficient to determine a
plane. From a geologic perspective, however, all data pair correlations can be used to
construct a plane. In this interpretation, the plane that gives rise to the maximum total
correlation when all possible pairs are considered is used. This best fit plane may or may
not pass through the actual peaks on the individual correlation curves, especially when the
detected events relate to a non-planar surface. The logic is to construct a correlation
surface with the axes being the shifts in all directions for all pads correlated, and the tilt
of the surface is the highest total correlation coefficient of each discrete shift along all
axes.

When tools with four or more electrodes are used, all possible correlations are considered.
The correlations fram the 8 electrode SHDT are computed and are searched for the plane
that gives rise to the maximum total correlation when all possible electrode pairs are
considered.

A quality coefficient is assigned to each computed dip plane that is related to both the
planarity of the event and the similarity of resistivity response (likeness) of each electrode:

Processing parameters for the 26/8-1 weil were selected after examining a playback of the
raw dipmeter data. As no supporting geologic information was available for use in
selecting correlation parameters, a relatively large search angle of 60° was used in the
event that high-angle structural dips were present. The correlation parameters used for
final processing were 4.0 ft x 2.0 ft x 60°, with the search angle oriented with respect to
the borehole. These parameters appear to give excellent results for structural interpretation
as well as an indication of many stratigraphic features.

No filtering of "mathematical" dips (dips which are artifacts of the processing technique
and have magnitudes near the maximum search angle} was deemed necessary and all
computed dips with correlation coefficients greater than 0.30 have been included in the
various plots and interpretations.

Wl. Dip Azimuth Vector Plot: Interval 6690 - 10032 ft.
(Figure 1 and Enciosure 4)

The Dip Azimuth Vector or "DAV" plot is a continuous vector plot of the azimuth
data from successive correlations of decreasing depth. The DAV is generated by
adding each azimuth vector to the subsequent vector on a horizontal plane.
Although the vector addition is for azimuth only (no dip magnitude), the resultant
plot can highlight structural changes such as faults and unconformities, as well as
depositional breaks and flow directions. The Dip Azimuth Vector plot (Enclosure 4
and Figure 1) for the entire studied interval of 6690 - 10032 ft. shows several slight
changes in dip direction. These occur at depths of approximately 9085, 9590, and
9810 ft. The structural and/or mean dip of the four intervals bounded by these DAV
breaks is quantified statistically using Schmidt polar plot analyses.

Structural dip may be interpreted from a statistical analysis of the data. After an
initial study of the tadpole plots and the Dip Azimuth Vector plot, Schmidt polar
plots were constructed for each of the intervals listed above. A first iteration mean
dip computation is performed for each interval. A second iteration mean dip
computation is then made using only the data points that lie within a specified cone
angie of the first-iteration mean dip. The result of this second iteration in low- |
energy sediments is interpreted as the best estimate of mean structural dip.

DISCUSSION OF RESULTS

Traditionally, structural dip is interpreted from the minimum consistent dip in low-energy |
sediments that are assumed to have been deposited with horizontal bedding and not to
have undergone any soft-sediment deformation. However, since much of the studied
interval appears to consist of either channel-type sandstone or shales in close vertical
proximity to channel-type sands, sedimentary dips in the sandstones and drape features
in the overlying shales may. figure prominently in the apparent structural dip. For this
reason, a Statistical approach has been used to interpret structural dip, using Schmidt polar
plots to define an iterative mean dip in selected intervals of the logged section.

The relatively coarse 4 ft. x 2 ft. structural processing reveals numerous clear “red” (dip .
increasing with depth) and “blue“ (dip decreasing with depth) patterns within the various

sand bodies. These sedimentary dip patterns are likely to be the product of channel fill and

current bedding features. A finer-resolution stratigraphic processing, coupled with

structural dip removal should provide good insight into the orientation of fluvial channel

axes and sediment transport directions. .

Breakout-orientation plots (directionally-oriented differential calipers) from multi-arm
calipers have long been used in the Carboniferous fields in the Southern North Sea and
onshore Northern Europe to determine the orientation of the local horizontal tectonic stress.
As there appears to be differential breakout in the sands in the lower section of the studied
interval, such a plot is recommended.
